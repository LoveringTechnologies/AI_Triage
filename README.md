Level 1: Resuscitation (Sickest)
These patients have conditions that are immediate threats to life or limb and require aggressive, immediate interventions.
Cardiovascular: Cardiac arrest, pre-arrest (severe hypoperfusion), or patients with return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC).
Respiratory: Respiratory arrest, severe respiratory distress (O2 saturation <90%), or airway compromise.
Neurological: Unconscious (GCS 3–9) or actively seizing.
Trauma: Major trauma with shock, traumatic amputation of an extremity, or chemical burns covering ≥25% of the body.
Obstetrics: Pregnancy ≥20 weeks with presenting fetal parts or a prolapsed cord.
Mental Health: Violent/homicidal behavior with imminent harm to self or others.
Level 2: Emergent
These patients have potential threats to life, limb, or function and require rapid medical intervention.
Cardiovascular: Chest pain with cardiac features and borderline perfusion (e.g., tachycardia with low blood pressure) or severe hypertension (SBP ≥220 or DBP ≥130) with symptoms.
Respiratory: Moderate respiratory distress (O2 saturation <92%) or a foreign body obstruction with stridor or drooling.
Neurological: Altered level of consciousness (GCS 10–13), sudden/severe headache ("worst ever"), or acute stroke symptoms with onset <3.5 hours.
Trauma: Penetrating injuries to the head, chest, or abdomen; major burns (≥25% body area); or neurovascular compromise of an extremity.
Gastrointestinal: Active or significant vomiting of blood (hematemesis) or large rectal bleeds.
Other: Severe pain (8–10/10 scale), severe sepsis (looks septic), or uncontrolled epistaxis (nosebleed).
Level 3: Urgent
These patients have conditions that could potentially progress to a serious problem; they often have normal vital signs but significant discomfort.
Cardiovascular: Chest pain with non-cardiac features or hypertension with symptoms (SBP 200–220 or DBP 110–130).
Respiratory: Mild to moderate respiratory distress (O2 saturation 92–94%).
Neurological: Seizures that have resolved with a normal level of alertness or stroke symptoms that started ≥3.5 hours ago.
Gastrointestinal: Small amounts of "coffee-ground" emesis (vomit) or small rectal bleeds.
Pain: Moderate acute central pain (4–7/10) or severe chronic central pain.
Pediatrics: Inconsolable infants with stable vital signs or children with a persistent cough who appear distressed.
Level 4: Less Urgent
These patients have conditions related to age, distress, or potential for complications that benefit from intervention or reassurance.
Trauma: Minor burns (<5% body area), simple lacerations requiring sutures, or upper extremity injuries.
Gastrointestinal: Constipation with mild pain (<4/10) or minor rectal bleeding.
Neurological: Chronic confusion with no change from the patient's usual state.
Other: Urinary tract infection symptoms (mild dysuria), mild anxiety, or mild hypothermia with normal vital signs.
Level 5: Non-Urgent
These are minor or chronic conditions that pose no immediate risk to the patient and where investigations or interventions could be delayed.
Trauma: Minor contusions, abrasions, or lacerations not requiring closure.
Respiratory: Sore throat or upper respiratory illness with no respiratory symptoms.
Gastrointestinal: Diarrhea with no dehydration and normal vital signs.
Other: Minor bites with mild pain or a request for a dressing change
